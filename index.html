Dim Light Melatonin Onset (DLMO) and Functional Connectivity in Later-Life Adults

Connor Baggot1 · Meina Zhang, PhD, RN2 · Karin Hoth, PhD3 · Navya Spurthi Thatikonda, MD, PhD · Eric Axelson, MS · Vincent Magnotta, PhD · Chooza Moon, PhD, RN2

1 University of Iowa College of Liberal Arts & Sciences  ·  2 College of Nursing, University of Iowa  ·  3 Carver College of Medicine, University of Iowa

Summary

What we found

We measured the internal body clock of 50 cognitively healthy older adults (median age ≈68) using Dim Light Melatonin Onset (DLMO) — the gold-standard marker of circadian timing — and mapped resting-state functional connectivity with MRI. Adults with a later circadian rhythm (later DLMO) showed stronger connectivity specifically within the dorsal Salience Network (SN-B), the system that shifts attention between inward and outward focus. The effect was network-specific: of 10 connections tested, only this one was significant — default-mode and hippocampal connections were unchanged. Because the salience network is among the networks affected early in Alzheimer’s-related decline, circadian timing may be an early, network-specific signal of how the aging brain changes.

Later DLMO → stronger within–Salience-Network-B connectivity — r = 0.37, p = 0.008 (age/sex-adjusted r = 0.40, p = 0.004; FDR q = 0.04; n = 50).
